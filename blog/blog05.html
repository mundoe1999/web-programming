Blog 05: Interconnected

March 2nd, 2020

Readings:
  • The Cute Cat Theory of Digital Activism
  • i had a couple of drinks and woke up with 1000 nerds

Interconnectedness on the internet is a massive deal. It creates a beautiful social link between two people that raise awareness, meaningfulness, and insightfulness of the people around them. However, governments want to keep these people segregated so that it becomes easier to control the population. This is somewhat the readings focus on, in ways how people are able to connect with each other. The cute cat theory reading discusses how for very arbitrary reasons, people are connected together. The "cute cat" theory is that if a website is used by a large majority of people, a government cannont block the entire website for having some content that does not showcase the government in a good light. That's how activists are able to get away with posting their content where dissent is suppressed, as they are able to post their activism in popular sites that if taken down, the large majority of people will be in a uproar, and increase awareness of the issues that activists wanted to point out. Everyone is connected to the website, and collectively they will fight back if they lose access to it.

The second reading, more lightheartedly, focuses on the creation of these connections between users. It is a very simple UNIX machine where everyone is allowed to do whaterver they want. However, what I find amusing is how these people just naturally clumped up together and started working in creating "infrastructure" and collaborating with each other. I say "infrastructure" as in, they were building links to each other, seeing the most recently updated personal site, and whatnot. Everyone was working to make this personal space their own and leaving their own footprint for the website. This reminds me of the days that I used to play in a political minecraft server called Civcraft. It was essentially an anarchy server (meaning that there were no rules with the exception of using game exploits or hacking and doxxing). Nobody had the obligation to help each other and work collaboratively, but they did. People built these massive nations, created large tunnels and rail systems connecting each major hub, and each civilization had their own culture and eccentiricities that made it unique. This reading felt very much like that, and I felt nostalgic over that entire wonder.
